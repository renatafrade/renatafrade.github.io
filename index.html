Renata Loureiro Frade, PhD

I am an interdisciplinary researcher working at the intersection of Communication Sciences, Information Science and Digital Technologies, with a focus on technology, Human‚ÄìComputer Interaction (HCI), transmedia, gender, feminism and society. My work combines critical theory, participatory methodologies and social impact, examining how digital technologies shape human interaction, power relations and processes of inclusion and exclusion.

Over the past decade, my research has focused on the leadership, organisation and communication practices of women-in-technology communities in Brazil and Portugal. This trajectory is grounded in a hybrid professional path that bridges academia, the publishing industry, science journalism and technology activism, allowing empirical contexts to inform theory-driven research.

I began my undergraduate studies in Social Communication at the Pontifical Catholic University of Rio de Janeiro in the same year the World Wide Web became publicly accessible. Despite having no computer or internet access at home, I enrolled in an advanced internet training course at one of Latin America's most sophisticated computing laboratories. Without prior digital literacy, I became one of the first people to communicate via the internet in Brazil‚Äîan experience that decisively shaped my commitment to studying communication and technology, as well as the social implications of digital platforms, the web and mobile devices.

Before my doctorate, I maintained a parallel academic and professional career, strategically using professional environments as sites for data collection and applied research. As a Science and Health journalist for major Brazilian newspapers (Extra and O Globo), I developed strong expertise in science communication, translating complex scientific information for socially diverse and underserved audiences. I also worked as a columnist on women in technology for M√≠dia Ninja, the largest independent media outlet in Latin America, exploring themes that later became central to my doctoral research.

I hold a Master's degree in Letters (UERJ) and have nearly two decades of experience in the academic and professional publishing sector. One of the most significant outcomes of this trajectory is the book Technofeminism: Multi and Transdisciplinary Contemporary Views of Women in Technology, which I conceived, co-edited and co-authored during my PhD. I mapped and analysed 247 women-in-technology communities in Brazil and Portugal, developing organisational and communication models, a transmedia campaign, and a feminist activist technology prototype, using qualitative methods, digital ethnography, participatory design and HCI.
